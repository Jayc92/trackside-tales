TracksideBrewing Co.

A craft brewery built on the belief that every great beer should come with a story worth telling.

OUR STORY

More than a brewery.

Trackside Brewing Co. is rooted in the Lehigh Valley — its people, its industry, and the stories that built this corner of America. We believe the history of a place belongs to the people who live in it, and that great beer is an honest way to share it.

We brew approachable craft beer for the everyday pour, a rotating Trackside Tales series tied to the real figures who shaped our region, and a full non-alcoholic lineup because everyone at the table deserves a beer that was made with care.

WHAT WE BELIEVE

How we brew.

01
Story First
Every Trackside Tales beer is tied to a real, researched piece of Lehigh Valley history. No invented lore. Real people, real places, real dates.
02
Community Over Scale
We partner with local charities, donate a percentage of select beer sales to Valley causes, and collaborate with neighboring breweries rather than compete with them.
03
Inclusive by Design
A full non-alcoholic program from day one — not an afterthought. Our N/A beers are brewed with the same care as the rest of the lineup.
04
Honest History
We tell the Lenape story alongside the colonial one. We don't simplify the history of this valley — we tell it whole.

BETHLEHEM, PA · EST. 1868

The Wooden MatchA Station That Stayed

You're standing inside a building that has served this city for more than 150 years.

BUILT 1868 · LINE CENTRAL RR OF NEW JERSEY · LAST TRAIN AUG 18, 1967
BEFORE THE WOODEN MATCH

Built in 1868.

Long before the craft beer and the live music, this building had a different job. It was a train station — a stop on the Central Railroad of New Jersey, the line that connected Bethlehem to the wider world during the height of America's rail era.

Constructed in 1868 and now approaching 160 years old, the building watched Bethlehem grow from a working iron town into the industrial heart of the Lehigh Valley. Passengers walked through these doors on their way to Philadelphia, New York, and the coalfields beyond.

A working station at the center of a working city.

A STATION WITH STORIES

Two presidents spoke from here.

During the age of the whistle-stop campaign — when candidates crossed the country by rail and addressed voters from the back platform of a train — Bethlehem's Central Railroad station was a natural stop.

  • Theodore Roosevelt spoke from a train car at this very station
  • Harry S. Truman did the same during his own whistle-stop campaign
  • Ordinary passengers passed through by the thousands, every day, for nearly a century

Passenger service continued here until August 18, 1967 — the final train, the end of an era. Across the Lehigh River, the rival Lehigh Valley Railroad ran its own line. For decades, the two companies competed for freight, for passengers, and for the identity of the valley.

FROM DEPOT TO GATHERING PLACE

A building that kept working.

When the trains stopped running, the building didn't. In 1976, the first floor reopened as The Lehigh Street Depot — a restaurant that honored the space's railroad roots in name and atmosphere.

It was later renamed Main Street Depot, and for decades it remained a Bethlehem landmark. The restaurant closed in 2010, leaving the old station quiet for the first time in over a century.

Then came The Wooden Match — bringing craft beer, gastropub dining, cigars, and live music back into a space built for gathering.

The trains are gone. The gathering never stopped.

STILL A GATHERING PLACE

History you can sit inside.

You can still feel the station in this building — in the bones of the walls, in the long wooden bar where travelers once waited for the next train east.

Trackside Brewing is here because the stories we tell are the same stories this building has carried since 1868. The founders, the railroad men, the workers who built the Valley — they all passed through here. Now you're the one sitting at the bar.

One hundred and fifty years of footsteps.
Yours are the latest.

BETHLEHEM, PA · RAIL HISTORY

The TracksRun Through Here

Every Trackside Tale is rooted in a real place. You're in one of them.

THE AGE OF RAIL IN BETHLEHEM

A city made by trains.

In the second half of the 19th century, Bethlehem ran on rail. Coal from the anthracite fields moved south. Iron — and later, steel — moved out. People, goods, and ideas moved in. The tracks didn't just pass through the city. They built it.

The station you're sitting in was part of that story. Constructed in 1868 as a stop on the Central Railroad of New Jersey, it served Bethlehem during the height of American rail travel — and through the decades of industrial growth that followed.

For nearly a century, this was how the world reached Bethlehem.

TWO RAILROADS, ONE VALLEY

A rivalry across the river.

Bethlehem didn't have one railroad. It had two.

The Central Railroad of New Jersey, which ran through this station, was one side of the story. On the opposite bank of the Lehigh River ran its fierce competitor — the Lehigh Valley Railroad, which stretched from Mauch Chunk down to Easton and carried much of the coal that fueled the industrial Northeast.

Two companies. Two sets of tracks. One valley.

  • The Central Railroad of New Jersey — station built 1868, ran on the north side of the Lehigh
  • The Lehigh Valley Railroad — founded by Asa Packer, ran on the south side
  • For decades, they competed for freight, passengers, and the identity of the region
A STATION THAT SAW HISTORY

Presidents, workers, and everyone between.

During the whistle-stop era, presidential candidates crossed the country by rail and spoke to voters from the back platforms of their train cars. Theodore Roosevelt and Harry S. Truman both addressed Bethlehem from this station.

But the station wasn't built for presidents. It was built for the thousands of ordinary passengers who used it every day — steelworkers, merchants, students, soldiers coming home, families heading out. A working stop on a working line.

Passenger service ended here on August 18, 1967. The rails went quiet. But the building stayed.

THE PEOPLE BEHIND THE BEERS

Every pour has a name.

Packer Pilsner is named for the man whose railroad ran across the river from where you're sitting. W.A. Lager honors the founder of Allentown, a few miles west. The Wooden Match Amber Ale is named for the building that pours it \u2014 an 1868 Bethlehem train station that became our taproom home.

These aren't invented characters. They're the people who built the place around you — and the Trackside Tales series is how we keep telling their stories.

The trains moved through here for a hundred years.
The stories stayed.
